# The Developer Journey

A practical guide for aspiring developers — what to learn, what to avoid, projects to build, interview preparation, deployment knowledge, and lessons from my own journey into software engineering.

## Before You start

I’m not a writer, and this probably won’t sound extremely technical or perfectly polished. This is simply the story of how I navigated the software development jorney, the problems I ran into, the architecture decisions I made, and the lessons I learned while trying to make everything work in life.

## Why I Created This

If you’re reading this, you might wonder why I’m writing all of this in the first place.

The answer is simple: this is everything I wish someone had told me when I started my journey.

Software development can feel overwhelming in the beginning. There are thousands of tutorials, endless technologies, and everyone online somehow seems ten steps ahead of you. Most people only share the polished version. The successful deployment, the clean architecture diagram, the final product. But very few talk about the confusion, the bad decisions, the deployment failures, the debugging at 3 AM, or the moments where you genuinely question whether you understand anything at all.

### _This blog is my attempt to document those parts too._

In some ways, it feels like writing a guide for my younger self. The version of me who was just getting started, trying to understand how real systems are built. And honestly, if I ever got a time machine, I’d probably still choose software development again. Because despite all the frustration, it’s beautiful. The ability to build things from nothing, solve problems, connect systems together, and slowly watch an idea turn into something real is still one of the most rewarding things I’ve experienced. So this is for anyone currently at the beginning of their journey.

### My story and what a beginner should learn from my mistake

When I started my journey into computer science, I was completely stuck in tutorial hell. Every day I watched countless YouTube videos about the “perfect roadmap.” One video would tell me to start with C++, another would say Java is the best choice, while someone else insisted that Python was all I needed. I spent more time searching for the “right way” to start than actually starting. And honestly, that confusion can drain your motivation very quickly. You begin to feel like everyone has figured things out except you. But over time, I realized something important: the language is not the thing that makes you a programmer. You can start with almost any language. C++, Java, Python, JavaScript. It genuinely does not matter as much as people make it seem. What matters is consistency and practice. Watch tutorials if they help you. Read blogs. Follow documentation. Learn in whatever way feels comfortable to you. But don’t become only a consumer of content. Open your editor and code alongside what you learn. Pause the video. Try things on your own. Break your code. Fix it again. That messy process of building, failing, and trying again is where real learning happens. Another thing I wish someone had told me earlier: move quickly. Don’t spend months trying to master every beginner topic perfectly before building something. Learn the basics, then start creating small projects. You will understand concepts much faster when you actually use them. Programming becomes a lot more enjoyable once you stop chasing the “perfect roadmap” and start focusing on progress instead. If you are starting you maybe start with a calculator. Write code for fibonacci series.
